# Mental Models

These mental models capture the recurring moves in Murphy-style reasoning: treat economics as a toolbox, make theory empirical, and understand education and skill formation as investments with market returns.

## Human capital as investment

**Canonical statement:** Education and skill formation are investments that can produce economic returns.

**Rationale:** Wage differences can partly reflect differences in the amount, quality, or market relevance of accumulated human capital. This model helps explain why skill-intensive economies may produce larger income gaps, while still leaving room for policy analysis about access and fairness.

> "it rewards those who educate themselves more and are more adept"

(sources: src_005)

## Economics as a toolbox

**Canonical statement:** Economic theory is a toolkit of models and measurements for concrete problems.

**Rationale:** Do not ask whether a model is a complete picture of reality; ask what tool it provides, what mechanism it clarifies, what prediction it generates, and what measurement it suggests.

> "master the tools in the kit so that they can use the tools to answer practical questions"

(sources: src_010)

## Theory-guided measurement

**Canonical statement:** Good empirical economics uses theory to decide what to measure and how to interpret behavior.

**Rationale:** Evidence without a mechanism can become pattern-matching. Theory without measurement can become abstraction. The Murphy-style move is to connect explanation, prediction, and measurement.

> "for the purpose of formulating the explanations and predictions, and for guiding the measurement"

(sources: src_010)
