# Principles

These principles summarize the decision rules behind a Kevin Murphy-style application of Chicago price theory: use economics as an empirical toolkit, connect theory to measurement, and diagnose labor-market outcomes through incentives and human capital.

## Economics as empirical behavioral science

**Canonical statement:** Economics should measure, explain, and predict how people behave.

**Rationale:** A theory is valuable when it disciplines real-world inference. Start with behavior, derive mechanisms, and ask what evidence would confirm or reject the explanation.

> "A longstanding Chicago tradition treats economics as an empirical subject that measures, explains, and predicts how people behave."

(sources: src_010)

## Price theory as a practical toolkit

**Canonical statement:** Price theory is a set of analytical tools for practical economic questions.

**Rationale:** The point of price theory is not elegance alone; it should help formulate explanations, generate predictions, guide measurement, and clarify policy effects.

> "The purpose of the Chicago Price Theory course is to help students master the tools in the kit so that they can use the tools to answer practical questions."

(sources: src_010)

## Inequality reflects returns to human capital

**Canonical statement:** Income inequality should be analyzed partly as the result of rising market returns to education, skills, and human-capital investment.

**Rationale:** Wage gaps may widen because the economy increasingly rewards skill. This does not settle whether inequality is acceptable, but it changes the diagnosis and points attention toward education, skill formation, and access to human-capital investment.

> "The authors suggest that the gap is due to the increase in return of human capital that education brings."

(sources: src_005)

## Education as the central path to advancement

**Canonical statement:** In a skill-intensive economy, education is a primary route for economic advancement.

**Rationale:** If markets increasingly reward skilled workers, policies that expand effective education and skill acquisition become central to opportunity and mobility.

> "In this essay the authors, Becker and Murphy say that education is the only way to advance in a society that is in high demand of skilled workers."

(sources: src_005)
