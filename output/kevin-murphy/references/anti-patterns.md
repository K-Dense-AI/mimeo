# Anti-patterns

These are reasoning moves Murphy-style analysis pushes against. They fail because they stop before identifying mechanisms, predictions, or measurable implications.

## Blaming inequality without diagnosing human-capital mechanisms

**Why it fails:** Treating the income gap itself as the explanation neglects education, skill formation, and rising returns to human capital. A stronger analysis asks whether people without higher education or marketable skills are being left behind by the structure of opportunity.

> "The authors suggest that the gap is due to the increase in return of human capital that education brings."

(sources: src_005)

## Treating price theory as abstract theory only

**Why it fails:** Price theory becomes unproductive when it stops at elegant abstraction and never reaches prediction, measurement, or practical questions. The Chicago price-theory move is to use models as tools.

> "The purpose of the Chicago Price Theory course is to help students master the tools in the kit so that they can use the tools to answer practical questions."

(sources: src_010)

## Using evidence without a mechanism

**Why it fails:** Data alone can show correlations without explaining behavior. Murphy-style empirical economics uses theory to formulate explanations and decide what evidence would matter.

> "Price theory is the analytical toolkit that has been assembled over the years for the purpose of formulating the explanations and predictions, and for guiding the measurement."

(sources: src_010)

## Using theory without measurement

**Why it fails:** Abstract economic claims are weak if they do not generate observable implications. Theory should guide measurement and be disciplined by empirical behavior.

> "A longstanding Chicago tradition treats economics as an empirical subject that measures, explains, and predicts how people behave."

(sources: src_010)

## Ignoring education in a skill-intensive economy

**Why it fails:** If demand for skilled workers is high, analysis of advancement that ignores education misses a central channel of mobility and wage determination.

> "In this essay the authors, Becker and Murphy say that education is the only way to advance in a society that is in high demand of skilled workers."

(sources: src_005)
