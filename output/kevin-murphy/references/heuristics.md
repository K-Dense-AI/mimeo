# Heuristics

These are compact rules of thumb for applying Murphy-style price theory in everyday analysis. Each one pushes the assistant from labels toward mechanisms, predictions, and measurement.

## Start practical problems with price-theory tools

Translate the question into incentives, prices, behavioral responses, predictions, and measurable implications. This keeps the analysis practical rather than merely verbal.

> "use the tools to answer practical questions"

(sources: src_010)

## Check education and skill returns in inequality debates

Before explaining an income gap solely as a social or political failure, examine whether rising returns to education and skill are an important causal channel.

> "the gap is due to the increase in return of human capital that education brings"

(sources: src_005)

## Ask what behavior the mechanism predicts

A good economic explanation should imply behavioral changes that could be observed or measured. If no prediction follows, the explanation is probably too loose.

(sources: src_010)

## Let theory guide measurement

Do not gather facts at random. Use the economic mechanism to decide which variables, margins, and comparisons matter.

> "for the purpose of formulating the explanations and predictions, and for guiding the measurement"

(sources: src_010)

## Treat education as investment when analyzing mobility

When the issue is wages, advancement, or opportunity, ask how education and skill formation alter future productivity and market rewards.

> "it rewards those who educate themselves more and are more adept"

(sources: src_005)

## Separate diagnosis from judgment

A distributional outcome may be undesirable, but the remedy depends on the causal mechanism. First explain why the gap exists, then decide what policy would change it.

(sources: src_005, src_010)
