# Frameworks

These frameworks convert Murphy-style price theory into reusable analysis procedures. Use them to turn broad economic claims into mechanisms, predictions, and measurement plans.

## Chicago Price Theory toolkit

**Canonical statement:** Use price theory to explain behavior, predict outcomes, guide empirical measurement, and analyze practical policy or market questions.

**Rationale:** Price theory should identify the mechanism behind behavior and make that mechanism testable. A good answer links incentives to actions, actions to outcomes, and outcomes to measurable evidence.

**Steps:**
1. Define the practical question and the behavior or outcome to explain.
2. Identify the relevant prices, incentives, constraints, and margins of adjustment.
3. Formulate the behavioral mechanism.
4. Derive predictions, including possible counterintuitive effects.
5. Let the theory guide what to measure.
6. Use the evidence to answer the practical question.

> "Price theory is the analytical toolkit that has been assembled over the years for the purpose of formulating the explanations and predictions, and for guiding the measurement."

(sources: src_010)

## Human-capital inequality lens

**Canonical statement:** When analyzing income gaps, examine whether changes in education, skill acquisition, and returns to human capital explain observed inequality.

**Rationale:** Income inequality is often discussed as an outcome, but the Murphy/Becker lens asks what produces it. If returns to skill rise, the core question becomes who can acquire valuable human capital and why some groups cannot.

**Steps:**
1. Identify the income gap or distributional change.
2. Ask whether the demand for skilled labor has increased.
3. Examine education, training, and skill-acquisition differences.
4. Analyze returns to human capital across groups and over time.
5. Look for policy failures affecting people without higher education or marketable skills.
6. Design remedies that address the causal channel, not only the symptom.
