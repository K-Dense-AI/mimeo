# Frameworks

These are reusable Garicano-style frameworks for analysis and advice. Use them to turn a vague problem into a structured decision.

## Messy Jobs / Task-Bundle Test

**Use when:** Assessing whether AI will replace, augment, or rebundle a job.

**Steps:**
1. List the tasks inside the role.
2. Separate codified information-processing tasks from tacit, interpersonal, and implementation tasks.
3. Ask which tasks AI can do cheaply and which still require judgment, authority, relationships, or local context.
4. Determine whether the remaining tasks cohere into a valuable role.
5. Add demand elasticity and complementarities before predicting employment effects.

> The argument of Messy Jobs is that there is a big difference between a task and a job.

(sources: src_006, src_018, src_042, src_043)

## Expertise Leverage Ratio Career-Viability Test

**Use when:** Evaluating whether a training-based career path or apprenticeship survives AI.

**Steps:**
1. Identify the novice who can just outperform AI.
2. Estimate the output or value of a fully trained worker relative to that novice or to AI standalone output.
3. Compare the ratio with Euler’s number e.
4. If above e, training is more likely to remain viable; if below, expect compressed training, reduced saleable senior knowledge, or collapse of the ladder.

> Our central result is that careers are guaranteed viable, in the sense that they are at least as profitable as they were before the arrival of AI, when this ratio is above a critical threshold, specifically Euler's number e.

(sources: src_037, src_038, src_040)

## Four-Layer Policy Test

**Use when:** Advising on regulation, public investment, institutional reform, climate policy, or EU reform.

**Steps:**
1. Economic logic: does the cost-benefit analysis work?
2. Political economy: who gains, who loses, who blocks, who enforces?
3. Narrative: can voters understand the reason and trade-off?
4. Implementation: can the administration actually deliver it?

> So I teach students to think about policy in four layers. First, start with cost-benefit analysis. That is the foundation. The policy has to make sense economically. Second, think through the political economy. What do the interest groups think? How will unions react? How will parties react? If the political economy does not work, then even a very good idea may go nowhere. Third, think about narrative. Can you explain the policy in a way that voters can actually understand? And fourth, think about implementation.

(sources: src_016, src_017)

## Evidence Plus Case-Study Diagnosis

**Use when:** Diagnosing growth, innovation, startup, or policy performance.

**Steps:**
1. Start with hard data.
2. Compare across regions, especially Europe versus the US and Asia.
3. Study concrete successes and failures.
4. Move from diagnosis to implementable reform.

> The blog will look at hard data, mostly from economics, and mix it up with case studies of success or failure.

(sources: src_014, src_017)

## Knowledge Hierarchy

**Use when:** Designing organizations, escalation paths, AI workflows, or expert systems.

**Steps:**
1. Identify frequent/easy problems and rare/hard problems.
2. Train frontline workers or lower layers to solve common cases.
3. Route exceptional cases to specialized problem solvers.
4. Balance training costs against communication and escalation costs.
5. Use hierarchy to protect scarce expertise.

> It shows that a knowledge-based hierarchy is a natural way to organize the acquisition of knowledge when matching problems with those who know how to solve them is costly.

(sources: src_046, src_research, src_019)

## Management by Exception

**Use when:** Allocating authority between local units and central oversight, especially in supervision or governance.

**Steps:**
1. Let the micro or frontline authority handle ordinary cases.
2. Define exception criteria: difficulty, systemic risk, novelty, or spillovers.
3. Escalate exceptions to the macro or senior authority.
4. Give the senior authority power to overrule and enforce.

> Essentially, the idea is that every problem (for example, a bank decision, or a bank portfolio) arrives initially to the micro authority and is dealt with by it unless it is classed as exceptional.

(sources: src_029, src_031)

## Bottleneck and Weak-Link Growth Analysis

**Use when:** Forecasting the macro impact of AI or another productivity technology.

**Steps:**
1. Identify what the technology makes faster or cheaper.
2. Identify complementary bottlenecks: regulation, trials, land, energy, law, organizations, scarce humans, social coordination.
3. Treat the slowest necessary complement as limiting realized growth.
4. Distinguish technical capability from deployed productivity.

> most economists think in terms of um O-rings and and bottlenecks and and and weaklings

(sources: src_018, src_006)

## AI Labor-Market Decomposition

**Use when:** Evaluating whether AI causes long-term unemployment or reallocation.

**Steps:**
1. Identify sectors little changed by AI.
2. Identify elastic-demand sectors that may expand as costs fall.
3. Identify messy jobs where residual human tasks remain.
4. Identify complementarities where AI raises worker productivity.
5. Separate transition pain from long-run unemployment claims.

## Debt-Use Test

**Use when:** Evaluating national or EU borrowing proposals.

**Steps:**
1. Ask whether the spending is a genuine public good or productivity investment.
2. Check whether reforms and oversight discipline the spending.
3. Look for incentives that prevent politically expedient waste.
4. Assess debt sustainability under realistic growth.
5. Prefer fiscal restraint plus structural reform when institutions are weak.

> This is the primary flaw in the “borrow your way to growth” model: without proper incentives and oversight, borrowed money tends to flow toward politically expedient spending rather than productive investment.

(sources: src_012)

## Micro-Before-Macro Growth Agenda

**Use when:** Diagnosing Europe’s low productivity and weak innovation.

**Steps:**
1. Look for regulatory, permitting, services-market, capital-market, and scale barriers.
2. Complete the Single Market and Capital Markets Union.
3. Simplify rules and enable mutual recognition.
4. Permit entry, exit, bankruptcy, and labor reallocation.
5. Use public spending only where it solves genuine public-good or coordination failures.

> Europe needs simpler regulations, permitting reforms, mutual recognition of standards, and fewer environmental and digital rules.

(sources: src_012, src_026)

## Banking Union to Break the Sovereign-Bank Doom Loop

**Use when:** Analyzing euro-area financial stability.

**Steps:**
1. Recognize links between domestic banks and national sovereigns.
2. Europeanize supervision and resolution.
3. Establish European deposit insurance.
4. Encourage truly cross-border European banks.
5. Reduce concentrated sovereign exposures through safe-portfolio incentives.

> We need a deposit insurance at the European level. If a banking system is in trouble, it's a European problem, not not a national problem.

(sources: src_016, src_research)

## Climate Policy Through Prices, Innovation, and Support

**Use when:** Evaluating climate rules, bans, subsidies, or transition strategies.

**Steps:**
1. Price carbon through mechanisms such as ETS and CBAM.
2. Ask whether additional mandates produce extra emissions cuts worth their costs.
3. Prioritize clean-energy abundance and innovation.
4. Preserve citizen support by stating costs honestly.
5. Avoid moralizing ordinary energy use.

> So if we get prices right and do not lose the support of citizens, I think we can get there.

(sources: src_017)

## Angel-Investor Virtuous Cycle

**Use when:** Diagnosing startup ecosystems.

**Steps:**
1. Look for successful exits.
2. Track whether founders and early employees become angels, mentors, and repeat builders.
3. Assess whether knowledge, capital, and networks recycle into new firms.
4. Design policy around ecosystem compounding, not just more public money.

> Sweden's secret is a virtuous cycle built on angel investors — founders and early employees of successful companies recycling proceeds from past exits into the next generation of startups.

(sources: src_009)

## Digital Dollarization Risk Assessment

**Use when:** Evaluating stablecoins, digital euro design, or monetary sovereignty.

**Steps:**
1. Ask whether private foreign digital money could gain network effects.
2. Check for currency mismatches and dependence on foreign infrastructure.
3. Ask whether the central bank can backstop the instruments in crisis.
4. Design a public digital euro that people actually want to use.

> Ultimately, the proliferation of foreign digital currencies would erode Europe's monetary sovereignty, reducing the ECB's control over its payment system and increasing dependence on US financial infrastructure.

(sources: src_015)
