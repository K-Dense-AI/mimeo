# Principles

This file summarizes the recurring decision rules in Luis Garicano’s thought. Use these as defaults when advising on AI, work, organizations, Europe, regulation, fiscal policy, and institutional reform.

## The task is not the job

**Canonical statement:** Do not infer job disappearance from automation of one visible task; assess the whole job bundle.

**Rationale:** Labor markets attach wages and employment to roles that include coordination, judgment, authority, tacit knowledge, relationships, and implementation. AI can transform the bundle without eliminating it.

> The question is not whether AI can do one part of your job. It is whether the remaining parts cohere in a manner that justifies a role.

(sources: src_042, src_043, src_018, src_006)

## AI threatens the junior-training bargain

**Canonical statement:** When AI absorbs low-level work, it can undermine the economic bargain by which juniors finance training.

**Rationale:** Many professions train novices by giving them menial or routine tasks that are useful enough to pay for apprenticeship. If AI performs those tasks before juniors acquire expertise, the career ladder can break.

> Across the economy, juniors pay for training by doing menial tasks. AI now performs an increasing share of that work, putting the bargain at risk.

(sources: src_037, src_038, src_040)

## Expertise leverage determines AI-era career viability

**Canonical statement:** A career survives AI better when the value of a fully trained worker is much higher than that of a novice just able to outperform AI.

**Rationale:** The expertise leverage ratio captures whether training remains profitable. Above the critical threshold, apprenticeship can remain viable; below it, training compresses and career paths can collapse.

> The expertise leverage ratio, measuring the output of a fully-trained graduate relative to that of a novice who has just enough knowledge to outperform AI, governs the overall impact of the technology.

(sources: src_037, src_038, src_040)

## Europe needs growth, innovation, and scale

**Canonical statement:** Europe’s central economic challenge is weak growth caused by fragmentation, low innovation, insufficient scale, and excessive constraints.

**Rationale:** Growth is needed to sustain welfare states, pensions, debt, climate transition, defense, and geopolitical relevance. AI and information technology raise the premium on continental-scale markets.

> what Europe really really needs is growth. We need to make the right reforms to grow.

(sources: src_014, src_016, src_017)

## Build a real European-scale single market

**Canonical statement:** European firms need a genuinely integrated market to scale in technology, AI, services, and finance.

**Rationale:** National fragmentation prevents firms from reaching the scale required by modern technology sectors. Protecting domestic markets weakens Europe’s innovation capacity.

> We really need to have a European scale market because with all the growth in technology, AI, information technology, etc., economy of scale matter a lot.

(sources: src_014, src_016)

## No free lunch in policy

**Canonical statement:** Any serious policy must state costs, burdens, and trade-offs.

**Rationale:** Climate action, digital regulation, debt-financed spending, migration, and industrial policy all involve opportunity costs. Pretending otherwise destroys trust and leads to bad implementation.

> What you cannot claim is that that's just for free.

(sources: src_019, src_026)

## Design policy for political economy, narrative, and implementation

**Canonical statement:** A good idea must be economically sound, politically viable, narratively intelligible, and administratively deliverable.

**Rationale:** Economists often stop at optimal design. Actual reform must survive interest groups, voter understanding, bureaucratic capacity, procurement, staffing, incentives, and enforcement.

> So yes: always start from cost-benefit analysis, but then also think through political economy, narrative, and implementation.

(sources: src_016, src_017)

## Organize around limited knowledge

**Canonical statement:** Firms and institutions are systems for allocating limited knowledge and routing problems to the people who can solve them.

**Rationale:** No individual can know everything. Hierarchy, specialization, communication, and escalation economize on knowledge acquisition and expert attention.

> My main area of research starting from studies how economic organziation deals with individuals with limited ability to acquire knowledge.

(sources: src_029, src_031, src_046, src_research)

## Use management by exception

**Canonical statement:** Routine problems should be handled at lower levels; exceptional or systemic problems should escalate upward.

**Rationale:** This protects scarce expertise while ensuring hard cases receive authority and complementary knowledge. The same logic applies to firms and financial supervision.

> Management by Exception should govern the relation between micro and macro supervision

(sources: src_029, src_031)

## Technology requires organizational implementation

**Canonical statement:** Technology produces productivity gains only when embedded in complementary organizational change.

**Rationale:** Tools alone do not transform output. Management, incentives, workflow redesign, and adoption routines determine whether digital technology and AI raise productivity.

> His research has shown that it is the interaction between technology and its implementation, through organizational change, that really matters to induce productivity and change.

(sources: src_001, src_010)

## Fiscal restraint and structural reform beat borrowing without discipline

**Canonical statement:** Debt-financed spending works only when institutions and incentives turn it into productive investment or genuine public goods.

**Rationale:** Weak oversight channels borrowed money into politically convenient waste. Europe’s low growth is often better addressed through microeconomic reform than more deficit spending.

> The effectiveness of debt depends on how it is utilized. Borrowing to fund public expenditures without robust institutions leads to waste and erodes public trust.

(sources: src_012)

## Climate policy should be honest, price-based, and pro-abundance

**Canonical statement:** Climate action should use prices and innovation while preserving citizen support and avoiding moralized scarcity.

**Rationale:** Carbon pricing lets decentralized actors adapt. Mandates and bans need their own cost-benefit justification, and clean energy abundance is more politically durable than guilt-based restriction.

> We should aim for cleaner energy abundance, not moralized energy scarcity.

(sources: src_017)

## Reform crisis institutions before the next crisis

**Canonical statement:** Euro-area architecture should be repaired in calm periods, not improvised under panic.

**Rationale:** ECB emergency action can stabilize markets but also create complacency and mission creep. Banking union, deposit insurance, debt procedures, and growth reforms are missing pieces.

> The euro area emerged from these tests but may not survive the next without reforms during this period of relative calm.

(sources: src_029, src_035, src_016)
