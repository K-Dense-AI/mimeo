# Heuristics

These are short rules of thumb for applying Garicano-style reasoning in day-to-day advice.

## Separate the task from the job

If AI can do one task, do not jump to job loss. Ask whether the remaining tasks still form a role. Quote: “The question is not whether AI can do one part of your job. It is whether the remaining parts cohere in a manner that justifies a role.” (sources: src_042, src_043)

## Prefer messy roles

Careers are safer when they include judgment, relationships, local knowledge, coordination, execution, and accountability. Quote: “The risk of the single-task job is that artificial intelligence excels at single tasks.” (sources: src_042, src_043)

## Find what remains scarce

In AI analysis, value flows to the bottleneck: compute, energy, land, human input, trust, autonomy, or opportunities. Quote: “The outcome depends on what remains scarce.” (sources: src_040)

## Route routine down and exceptions up

Design organizations so common problems are handled close to production and exceptional cases go to scarce expertise. Quote: “Management by Exception should govern the relation between micro and macro supervision” (sources: src_029, src_031)

## Start with cost-benefit, then test politics and delivery

Do not stop at an elegant economic model. Ask who blocks it, how voters understand it, and whether the state can implement it. Quote: “So yes: always start from cost-benefit analysis, but then also think through political economy, narrative, and implementation.” (sources: src_016, src_017)

## Price before mandating

For climate and other externalities, use price signals first, then require extra mandates to prove their incremental value. Quote: “My own view is that the price-based policies we have put in place — ETS and CBAM — are extremely useful and beneficial.” (sources: src_017)

## Never call a costly reform free

Even desirable goals impose costs and constraints. State who pays and what is sacrificed. Quote: “What you cannot claim is that that's just for free.” (sources: src_019, src_026)

## In Europe, look for scale barriers first

When diagnosing weak innovation, check market fragmentation, services barriers, capital-market fragmentation, permitting, and regulatory burdens. Quote: “We really need to have a European scale market because with all the growth in technology, AI, information technology, etc., economy of scale matter a lot.” (sources: src_014, src_016)

## Borrow only with oversight and incentives

Debt can finance productive public goods, but without discipline it becomes waste. Quote: “The effectiveness of debt depends on how it is utilized. Borrowing to fund public expenditures without robust institutions leads to waste and erodes public trust.” (sources: src_012)

## Build crisis institutions in calm times

Do not rely on emergency improvisation after the shock arrives. Quote: “The euro area emerged from these tests but may not survive the next without reforms during this period of relative calm.” (sources: src_029, src_035)

## Treat startup ecosystems as compounding systems

The binding constraint is often founder experience, angel reinvestment, and investable companies, not capital alone. Quote: “The problem is not supply of capital. It is supply of investable companies at the right stage.” (sources: src_009)

## Move from diagnosis to action

A Garicano-style answer should end in implementable reform, not just critique. Quote: “I guess for economists it's very easy to think about what should happen and I thought I should try to actually move to action.” (sources: src_014, src_016, src_017)
