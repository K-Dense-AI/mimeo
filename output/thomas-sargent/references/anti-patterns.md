# Anti-Patterns

These are reasoning habits Sargent-style analysis pushes against. The corpus supplied no direct source IDs or quotes, so entries are unattributed syntheses.

## Treating expectations as fixed background conditions

**Why it fails:** Policies alter the forecasts that households, firms, and markets use to make decisions. Holding expectations fixed can turn an equilibrium problem into a misleading partial-equilibrium story.

## Ignoring the government budget constraint

**Why it fails:** Fiscal and monetary choices are connected across time. Spending, taxes, debt, and inflation cannot be analyzed as independent levers without tracing who ultimately pays and when.

## Extrapolating reduced-form correlations across regimes

**Why it fails:** A relationship estimated under one policy regime may not hold under another. If the intervention changes incentives or expectations, the old coefficient may not answer the new question.

## Confusing a solved model with reality

**Why it fails:** Solving a model shows what follows from assumptions. It does not prove the assumptions are true or that omitted mechanisms are irrelevant.

## Making discretionary promises without credibility

**Why it fails:** Private agents anticipate future incentives to renege, inflate, bail out, or relax constraints. Without credible commitment, announcements may have little or opposite effect.

## Optimizing narrowly under false precision

**Why it fails:** A policy that is optimal in a highly specific model can perform badly if the model is misspecified. High precision around weak assumptions creates fragility.

## Offering policy advice without an equilibrium mechanism

**Why it fails:** Advice that lists intended effects but not behavioral responses leaves out the core economic question: how agents respond when all constraints and expectations are mutually consistent.

## Treating central bank and treasury actions as separable in all states

**Why it fails:** Monetary outcomes can depend on fiscal backing, debt dynamics, and expected future surpluses. Institutional separation does not eliminate consolidated public-sector constraints.
