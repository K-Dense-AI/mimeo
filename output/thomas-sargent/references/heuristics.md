# Heuristics

These are compact rules of thumb for applying Sargent-style reasoning. They are synthesis statements; the provided corpus contained no source IDs or verbatim quotes.

## Start with the constraint

Before debating desirability, identify what is feasible over time. Budget constraints, resource constraints, and information constraints often decide which proposals are serious.

## Ask what agents expect

If a policy changes expected future prices, taxes, rates, or rules, those expectations are part of the current effect.

## Convert stories into states and laws of motion

A useful model says what variables summarize the present and how they evolve. If the story cannot be made recursive, it may be hiding the mechanism.

(sources: no corpus source ids supplied)

## Evaluate policy as a rule

Do not ask only, "What if we do this once?" Ask what happens when agents understand the rule and behave accordingly.

(sources: no corpus source ids supplied)

## Treat credibility as an equilibrium outcome

A promise is credible when incentives and institutions make carrying it out optimal or enforceable, not merely when officials announce it.

(sources: no corpus source ids supplied)

## Be suspicious of policy-invariant correlations

Historical correlations may be artifacts of the old regime. Ask whether the relationship survives the policy change being proposed.

(sources: no corpus source ids supplied)

## Locate the margin of adjustment

For every public promise, ask what eventually adjusts: taxes, spending, inflation, default, quantities, prices, or expectations.

(sources: no corpus source ids supplied)

## Prefer robust rules under model uncertainty

When plausible models disagree, avoid recommendations that work only if one fragile specification is exactly right.

(sources: no corpus source ids supplied)

## Separate model implication from value judgment

A model can clarify tradeoffs, but it does not by itself choose society's objective function. State where economics ends and values enter.
