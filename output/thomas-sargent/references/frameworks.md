# Frameworks

These frameworks translate Sargent-style macroeconomic reasoning into reusable procedures. They are synthesis frameworks based on his well-known intellectual domains; the provided corpus contained no source IDs.

## Recursive Policy Analysis

**Canonical statement:** Represent the problem recursively: define the state, controls, constraints, transition laws, and value functions, then compare policy rules over time.

**When to use:** Dynamic economic decisions: fiscal policy, monetary policy, debt sustainability, investment, regulation, insurance, pensions, or any choice where today's action changes tomorrow's feasible set.

**Steps:**
1. Identify state variables that summarize history relevant for future decisions.
2. Identify decision variables controlled by agents and policymakers.
3. Specify preferences, technologies, constraints, and shocks.
4. Write laws of motion for states.
5. Solve or reason through the equilibrium path.
6. Compare policies by welfare, feasibility, distribution, and robustness.

**Rationale:** Recursion prevents policy analysis from stopping at the first-round effect. It reveals how actions reshape future constraints and incentives.

## Expectations-and-Credibility Test

**Canonical statement:** A policy announcement matters only through the beliefs and incentives that make it credible in equilibrium.

**When to use:** Inflation stabilization, central bank commitments, fiscal consolidations, exchange-rate pegs, debt promises, regulation, or organizational commitments.

**Steps:**
1. State the announced rule or commitment.
2. Ask what agents believe about future implementation.
3. Identify the policymaker's future temptation to deviate.
4. Determine whether institutions, reputations, or constraints make the rule credible.
5. Predict private-sector responses under credible and non-credible scenarios.
6. Redesign the rule if the credibility constraint fails.

**Rationale:** Announcements without incentives are cheap talk. The analysis must connect words, institutions, and future payoffs.

## Lucas-Critique Policy Evaluation

**Canonical statement:** Do not assume historical reduced-form relationships remain stable after a policy regime changes.

**When to use:** Forecasting the effects of new taxes, monetary regimes, regulations, stimulus programs, price controls, or any intervention justified by past correlations.

**Steps:**
1. Identify the empirical relationship being used for prediction.
2. Ask which expectations, incentives, or constraints generated it historically.
3. Determine whether the proposed policy changes those primitives.
4. Replace the reduced-form prediction with a structural mechanism where possible.
5. State which parameters are assumed invariant and why.

**Rationale:** Policies can change behavior, and changed behavior can invalidate the empirical regularity used to justify the policy.

## Government Budget Constraint Trace

**Canonical statement:** Follow the government's intertemporal budget constraint before evaluating fiscal or monetary promises.

**When to use:** Debt sustainability, inflation risk, fiscal stimulus, entitlement reform, tax cuts, default risk, central-bank balance sheets, or public investment.

**Steps:**
1. Write the sources of funds: taxes, debt issuance, money creation, asset sales, or default.
2. Write the uses of funds: spending, transfers, interest, principal, bailouts.
3. Project how obligations evolve under different interest-growth-inflation assumptions.
4. Ask which adjustment margin is politically and economically credible.
5. Evaluate how private expectations respond.

**Rationale:** Fiscal and monetary outcomes are jointly determined. A debt path that looks harmless under one assumption can imply future taxes, inflation, or restructuring under another.

## Robustness Audit

**Canonical statement:** Stress-test the recommendation against plausible model misspecifications.

**When to use:** High-stakes policy decisions, fragile empirical evidence, novel environments, or strategic settings with uncertainty about preferences, shocks, and information.

**Steps:**
1. Name the benchmark model and its key assumptions.
2. List nearby alternative models that a reasonable skeptic would consider.
3. Identify which assumptions drive the recommendation.
4. Check whether the policy remains acceptable across alternatives.
5. Prefer robust rules when the best-fitting model is not decisively credible.

**Rationale:** The true model is rarely known. Robustness is a safeguard against overfitting a policy to a convenient abstraction.

## Econometric Identification Check

**Canonical statement:** Before treating evidence as causal, ask what variation identifies the mechanism and whether it is policy-invariant.

**When to use:** Empirical claims, forecasting models, treatment effects, macro regressions, and policy impact estimates.

**Steps:**
1. State the causal claim.
2. Identify the source of variation.
3. Ask whether expectations or selection contaminate the estimate.
4. Check whether the estimated relationship is structural or regime-specific.
5. Use the estimate only within the domain where the identification argument holds.

**Rationale:** Quantitative discipline requires more than data fitting. The estimate must answer the policy question being asked.
