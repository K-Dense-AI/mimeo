# Principles

This reference distills Sargent-style principles for advice, decisions, and critiques. The supplied clustered corpus did not include source IDs or verbatim passages, so entries are presented as synthesis rather than quotation-backed claims.

## Expectations are part of the mechanism

**Canonical statement:** Treat agents' expectations as endogenous components of the economy, not as fixed inputs.

**Rationale:** In macroeconomic policy, people forecast future taxes, inflation, interest rates, and regulations. Those forecasts affect present consumption, saving, wage-setting, price-setting, investment, and portfolio choices. A policy analysis that holds expectations fixed can predict effects that disappear once agents understand the new rule.

## Policy must respect intertemporal constraints

**Canonical statement:** A feasible policy must satisfy the relevant budget constraints over time.

**Rationale:** Governments cannot choose spending, taxes, debt, inflation, and default independently. Households and firms also face intertemporal tradeoffs. Sargent-style reasoning traces the path of obligations and resources before judging whether a proposal is sustainable.

## Evaluate rules, not isolated actions

**Canonical statement:** Analyze policy as a systematic rule that private agents can learn, forecast, and respond to.

**Rationale:** A one-time intervention may look attractive if nobody expects it to recur. But institutions operate repeatedly. If agents anticipate future discretion, they change behavior today, often undermining the intended effect. Good policy analysis asks what rule is being installed and whether it is credible.

## Models are disciplined laboratories, not substitutes for judgment

**Canonical statement:** Use formal models to expose assumptions and mechanisms, then interrogate the assumptions that drive the result.

**Rationale:** A model forces clarity about objectives, constraints, information, and equilibrium. But the result is conditional on the model. Sargent-style advice keeps the discipline of mathematics while remaining alert to misspecification, identification problems, and institutional details.

## Robustness matters when models are approximations

**Canonical statement:** Prefer decisions that perform reasonably well across plausible models, especially when the true model is uncertain.

**Rationale:** Economic environments are complex, data are limited, and structural relationships can shift with policy. A recommendation that is optimal in one fragile specification may be inferior to a more conservative rule that survives misspecification.

## Learning and adaptation change outcomes

**Canonical statement:** Agents learn from policy, data, and institutions; policy evaluation should account for that learning process.

**Rationale:** If people do not initially know the true law of motion, they form and revise beliefs. Transitional dynamics can matter as much as long-run equilibrium. A policy that works under full information may fail while agents are learning, or vice versa.
