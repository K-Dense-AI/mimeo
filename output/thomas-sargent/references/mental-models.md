## Rational Expectations

**Canonical statement:** Agents' forecasts should be modeled as consistent with the economic environment and policy rule they inhabit.

**Rationale:** People do not passively repeat past mistakes when policy regimes are understandable. They use available information, learn incentives, and adjust behavior. This does not mean perfect foresight; it means forecasts are part of equilibrium.

## Recursive Equilibrium

**Canonical statement:** Today's decisions depend on a state that summarizes relevant history and a rule that maps states into actions.

**Rationale:** Recursion makes dynamic problems tractable and forces clarity. It highlights which variables matter, how the future depends on the present, and what must be solved jointly.

## Policy Regime, Not Policy Move

**Canonical statement:** The regime governing future actions is often more important than the current action itself.

**Rationale:** A temporary tax, rate change, bailout, or price control has different effects depending on whether agents expect it to be repeated, reversed, or expanded. Regime expectations shape current behavior.

## Intertemporal Budget Constraint

**Canonical statement:** Debts and promises must eventually be financed through future primary surpluses, inflation, default, or asset transfers.

**Rationale:** The constraint may be postponed, disguised, or shifted across institutions, but it cannot be abolished. It links fiscal policy, monetary policy, and credibility.

## Time Consistency

**Canonical statement:** A plan chosen today may not be optimal for the policymaker to carry out tomorrow, and private agents anticipate that temptation.

**Rationale:** Dynamic incentives can make discretionary policy ineffective or inflationary. Institutions and rules matter because they alter future incentives and expectations.

## Robust Control

**Canonical statement:** When the model is uncertain, choose policies that guard against damaging misspecification rather than optimizing narrowly for a single model.

**Rationale:** Economic models are approximations. Robust control formalizes caution: ask how bad outcomes could be if the benchmark model is wrong in plausible ways.

## Learning Dynamics

**Canonical statement:** Agents may not know the true model; they estimate, update, and adapt as data arrive.

**Rationale:** Transitional dynamics matter. Beliefs formed during disinflations, crises, reforms, or new regimes can shape outcomes for long periods.

## Structural Versus Reduced-Form Relationships

**Canonical statement:** A reduced-form correlation is useful only if the underlying structural mechanism remains stable under the contemplated policy.

**Rationale:** Econometric patterns are generated by behavior under particular regimes. Change the rule, and the pattern may change too.
